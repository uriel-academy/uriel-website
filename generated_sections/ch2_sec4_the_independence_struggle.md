## Main Content

### The Rise of Nationalism
In the early 20th century, a new wave of African nationalism began to sweep across the continent. Educated Ghanaians, such as **Joseph Boakye Danquah** and **Kwame Nkrumah**, started questioning the legitimacy of British colonial rule. They believed that Ghana had the right to govern itself and determine its own future.

### The Formation of Political Parties
To advance their cause, Ghanaian leaders formed political parties to mobilize the masses and push for independence. The **United Gold Coast Convention (UGCC)**, founded in 1947, was one of the first major parties to emerge. However, it was the **Convention People's Party (CPP)**, led by Kwame Nkrumah, that would eventually lead Ghana to independence.

### Key Events in the Independence Struggle
The road to independence was marked by several pivotal events:

1. **1948 Riots**: Protests erupted in Accra after British soldiers opened fire on a peaceful demonstration, killing three Ghanaian World War II veterans. This event galvanized the independence movement.

2. **Positive Action Campaign**: In 1950, Kwame Nkrumah launched a campaign of civil disobedience, strikes, and boycotts to pressure the British government to grant Ghana independence.

3. **1956 Election**: The CPP won a decisive victory in the 1956 legislative election, paving the way for Ghana's independence.

### The Birth of a Nation
On March 6, 1957, Ghana officially gained its independence, with Kwame Nkrumah serving as its first Prime Minister. The nation celebrated with great fanfare, as the Black Star flag was raised high and the new national anthem, "God Bless Our Homeland Ghana," filled the air.

## Examples
1. **The Big Six**: The "Big Six" were six prominent leaders of the UGCC who played a crucial role in Ghana's independence struggle. They included J.B. Danquah, Kwame Nkrumah, Ebenezer Ako-Adjei, Edward Akufo-Addo, Emmanuel Obetsebi-Lamptey, and William Ofori Atta.

2. **The Accra Evening News**: Founded by Kwame Nkrumah in 1948, this newspaper served as a powerful tool for spreading the message of independence and mobilizing support for the CPP.

## Summary
- Ghana's independence struggle was driven by the rise of African nationalism and the formation of political parties like the UGCC and CPP.
- Key leaders such as J.B. Danquah and Kwame Nkrumah played pivotal roles in the fight for independence.
- The 1948 Riots, Positive Action Campaign, and 1956 Election were crucial events that led to Ghana's independence.
- On March 6, 1957, Ghana became the first sub-Saharan African nation to gain independence, with Kwame Nkrumah as its first Prime Minister.
- The independence struggle required great sacrifice and perseverance from the Ghanaian people.
- Ghana's independence served as an inspiration for other African nations seeking to break free from colonial rule.
