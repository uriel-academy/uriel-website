## Main Content

### Types of Electoral Systems in Ghana
Ghana primarily uses two electoral systems:
1. **First-Past-The-Post (FPTP)**: Used for parliamentary elections. Candidates compete in single-member constituencies, and the one with the most votes wins, even without a majority.
2. **Two-Round System**: Used for presidential elections. If no candidate gets over 50% in the first round, the top two face off in a second round.

### How First-Past-The-Post Works
In Ghana's 275 parliamentary constituencies, each party nominates one candidate. On election day, voters choose their preferred candidate. The candidate with the most votes in each constituency becomes the MP, even if they don't get a majority. This tends to favor large parties and can result in "wasted votes" if many votes go to runners-up.

Advantages of FPTP:
- Simple and easy for voters to understand
- Provides direct local representation
- Usually produces stable single-party governments

Disadvantages of FPTP:  
- Doesn't always reflect the popular vote share
- Can exclude smaller parties from parliament
- May discourage voter turnout in "safe seats"

### Presidential Elections and the Two-Round System
For presidential elections, Ghana uses a two-round system. To win in the first round, a candidate must receive:
- More than 50% of the total valid votes cast
- At least 25% of the valid votes cast in each of more than half of the regions

If no candidate meets these thresholds, a run-off is held between the top two candidates. This ensures the president has broad support.

### Impact on Governance and Democracy
Ghana's electoral systems have both shaped and reflected the country's democratic development:

- FPTP has contributed to a stable two-party system dominated by the NPP and NDC
- Presidential runoffs build consensus and legitimacy for the winner
- Losing parties have consistently accepted results, facilitating peaceful transitions
- High turnout (over 67% in 2020) suggests public faith in the electoral process

However, some argue for electoral reforms:
- Proportional representation could include more voices in parliament
- Anti-corruption measures and campaign finance regulations
- Strengthening institutions like the EC for efficiency and transparency

## Case Study: Cape Coast's Competitive Constituency
Cape Coast, the historic capital of the Central Region, illustrates the dynamics of FPTP. The Cape Coast South constituency has swung between the NPP and NDC recently:

- 2020: NDC's Kweku George Ricketts-Hagan defeated NPP's Ernest Arthur by just 1,000 votes 
- 2016: NPP's Barbara Asher Ayisi narrowly beat George Ricketts-Hagan
- 2012: Ricketts-Hagan edged out NPP's Comfort Ghansah

These close races reflect Cape Coast's politically engaged electorate, with campaign rallies, debates, and voter mobilization efforts. They also show how just a few votes can determine the winner under FPTP.

## Summary
- Ghana uses first-past-the-post for parliamentary elections and a two-round system for presidential elections
- FPTP means the candidate with the most votes in each constituency becomes MP
- Presidential candidates need over 50% of votes and regional distribution to win in the first round
- These systems have produced generally stable governments and peaceful transitions
- However, there are debates about potential electoral reforms to improve representation and governance

## Key Vocabulary

| Term                   | Definition                                                |
|------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------|
| Constituency           | An electoral area represented by a single MP               |
| First-past-the-post    | Electoral system where the candidate with the most votes wins     |
| Proportional representation | Electoral system where seats are allocated based on vote share |
| Safe seat              | A constituency where one party consistently wins by a large margin |  
| Swing constituency     | A constituency with close races and alternating winners    |
| Two-party system       | Political landscape dominated by two major parties        |
| Two-round system       | Electoral system with a runoff if no candidate gets a majority |
| Valid votes            | Votes properly cast for an eligible candidate             |
| Voter turnout          | Percentage of eligible voters who actually vote           |
| Wasted votes           | Votes cast for losing candidates under FPTP               |
