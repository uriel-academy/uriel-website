## Main Content
### Safety Symbols and Their Meanings
In a laboratory setting, safety symbols are used to communicate potential hazards and remind users of proper safety protocols. Some common safety symbols include:
- **Flammable**: Indicates substances that can easily catch fire, such as ethanol or kerosene.
- **Corrosive**: Represents substances that can cause damage to skin, eyes, or other materials, like sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) or sodium hydroxide (NaOH).
- **Toxic**: Denotes substances that can cause harm or death if inhaled, ingested, or absorbed through the skin, such as lead compounds or pesticides.
- **Biohazard**: Signifies the presence of biological materials that may contain infectious agents, like bacteria or viruses.

### Proper Handling and Use of Laboratory Equipment
To ensure safety and maintain the integrity of scientific experiments, it is essential to handle laboratory equipment properly. Some key guidelines include:
1. Always wear protective gear, such as gloves and safety goggles, when handling chemicals or biological materials.
2. Use tongs or heat-resistant gloves when handling hot objects, like beakers or test tubes heated by a Bunsen burner.
3. Clean and dry equipment thoroughly after each use to prevent contamination and maintain functionality.
4. Store equipment and chemicals in their designated locations, following proper labeling and organization protocols.

### Maintaining a Clean and Organized Laboratory
A clean and organized laboratory is crucial for safety, efficiency, and accuracy in scientific investigations. To maintain a proper laboratory environment:
- Keep work areas clutter-free and wipe down surfaces with disinfectant regularly.
- Dispose of waste materials, such as chemicals or biological samples, in the appropriate containers.
- Ensure proper ventilation to prevent the buildup of fumes or airborne particles.
- Store equipment and materials in labeled cabinets or shelves, grouping them by function or hazard level.

Practical Example: Creating a Makeshift Hand Sanitizer
In this experiment, students will learn how to create a simple hand sanitizer using locally available materials, emphasizing the importance of cleanliness in a laboratory setting.

Materials:
- 100 ml of 70% isopropyl alcohol (or ethanol)
- 10 ml of aloe vera gel
- 5 drops of tea tree essential oil (optional)
- A clean, empty bottle with a cap

Procedure:
1. In a clean beaker, measure out 100 ml of 70% isopropyl alcohol (or ethanol).
2. Add 10 ml of aloe vera gel to the alcohol and stir gently until well combined.
3. If desired, add 5 drops of tea tree essential oil for fragrance and additional antibacterial properties.
4. Pour the mixture into the clean, empty bottle and secure the cap tightly.
5. Label the bottle "Hand Sanitizer" and include the date of preparation.

## Worked Examples
1. Calculating the concentration of a solution:
   - A student prepares a sugar solution by dissolving 50 grams of sugar in 500 ml of water. Calculate the concentration of the solution in grams per liter (g/L).
   - Solution:
     * Concentration = mass of solute ÷ volume of solution
     * Concentration = 50 g ÷ 0.5 L
     * Concentration = 100 g/L

2. Measuring the volume of an irregularly shaped object:
   - A student wants to measure the volume of a small rock using the water displacement method. The student fills a graduated cylinder with 50 ml of water and drops the rock into the cylinder. The new water level is 65 ml. Calculate the volume of the rock.
   - Solution:
     * Volume of rock = final water level - initial water level
     * Volume of rock = 65 ml - 50 ml
     * Volume of rock = 15 ml (or 15 cm³)
