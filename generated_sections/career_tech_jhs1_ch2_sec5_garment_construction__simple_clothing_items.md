## Learning Objectives
After completing this section, students will be able to:
1. Accurately measure body dimensions and draft simple patterns for basic garments.
2. Cut fabric according to patterns, maintaining straight edges and correct seam allowances. 
3. Sew straight seams, curved seams, and hems using a sewing machine.
4. Attach zippers, buttons, and other fasteners securely and neatly.
5. Construct a simple shirt, skirt, shorts or trousers following a pattern.

## Introduction
Clothing is an essential part of our daily lives as Ghanaians. From the colorful kente cloth worn for special occasions, to the practical work uniforms of market vendors and shop attendants, to the stylish outfits created by local fashion designers - garments play an important role in Ghanaian society and culture. Learning basic sewing and garment construction skills empowers you to create and repair your own clothing. These valuable skills can also lead to careers as a tailor, seamstress, fashion designer or entrepreneur. Sewing your own clothes allows you to express your personal style, saves money compared to buying ready-made, and opens up opportunities to start a small business.

In this chapter, you will learn fundamental garment construction techniques and gain practical experience sewing simple clothing items. The skills of measuring, cutting, stitching seams, attaching fasteners, and finishing garments will serve you well whether making clothes for yourself and your family, or pursuing a career in the fashion industry. So thread your needle, start up your sewing machine, and let's begin exploring the world of garment construction!

### Taking Measurements and Creating Patterns
The first step in constructing a garment is taking accurate body measurements. Use a flexible measuring tape to measure key points like bust/chest, waist, hips, arm length, etc. Write down these measurements neatly.

[IMAGE: demonstrating how to measure bust, waist, hips on a model]

Using the body measurements, you will create a **pattern** for the garment. A pattern is like a template that you'll use to cut the fabric pieces. You can draft patterns from scratch or work from an existing commercial pattern. Be sure to include **seam allowances** of 1-1.5cm on all edges (except hems).

[IMAGE: a basic bodice pattern showing labeled pieces and seam allowances]

### Cutting Fabric and Stitching Seams
Once you have your pattern pieces ready, lay them out on the fabric and carefully cut around them with fabric scissors. Make sure to follow the **grain** of the fabric (the direction of the threads) and keep edges straight. Transfer all pattern markings to the fabric.

[IMAGE: fabric with pattern pieces pinned, showing cutting lines and markings]

Now you're ready to start stitching! Thread your sewing machine and set it to a straight stitch. Pin fabric pieces together, right sides facing, and stitch along the seam lines. Backstitch at the beginning and end of each seam to secure the threads. 

[IMAGE: hands pinning and sewing fabric at sewing machine]

Here are some tips for sewing neat, strong seams:
- Align fabric edges carefully and pin in place
- Sew slowly and steadily, guiding the fabric straight
- Backstitch 3-4 stitches at each end
- Press seams open with an iron 
- Finish raw edges with zigzag stitch or pinking shears
- Clip curved seams and corners before turning right side out

### Attaching Fasteners and Finishing Details
The final steps in garment construction involve attaching closures like zippers and buttons, and finishing hems and edges. Take your time with these detail steps, as they have a big impact on the final look of the garment.

Zippers:
1. Pin zipper to the seam allowance only, right sides together
2. Use a zipper foot attachment to stitch close to the zipper teeth
3. Fold fabric back over the stitching and topstitch along the fold 

[IMAGE: close-ups of pinning and stitching zipper, with finished zipper opening]

Buttons & Buttonholes:
1. Mark button placement on the overlap side
2. Make a small slit or use a buttonhole attachment to create buttonholes
3. Sew buttons securely on the underlap side, matching buttonhole positions

Hems & Edges:
- Fold raw edges under 0.5cm, then fold again 1-2cm depending on garment style. Pin in place
- Stitch close to the inner fold to create a neat hem
- For curved hems, make small snips in the seam allowance before folding to help fabric lie flat
- You can also use bias tape to bind raw edges for a decorative finish

## Practical Activity
Now it's your turn to practice these garment construction skills! Follow the steps below to sew a simple elastic-waist skirt:

Materials:
- 1 meter wax print cotton fabric 
- 2.5cm wide elastic, long enough to fit your waist comfortably
- Matching thread
- Basic sewing tools (machine, scissors, pins, measuring tape)

Instructions:
1. Measure your waist and desired skirt length. Add 3cm to the length for hem allowance.
2. Cut a rectangle of fabric the length measurement by your waist measurement plus 10cm (for seam allowance and ease).
3. Sew the short ends of the rectangle together with a 1.5cm seam allowance, right sides together, to form a tube. 
4. Fold over the top edge by 1.5cm and press. Fold over again by 3cm and press to create the elastic casing. Stitch close to the inner fold, leaving a 5cm opening.
5. Thread the elastic through the casing using a safety pin. Overlap the ends by 1cm and sew securely. Sew the opening in the casing closed.
6. Try on the skirt and adjust the gathers evenly. Trim the elastic if needed for a snug fit.
7. Fold up the bottom edge by 0.5cm and press. Fold up again by 2cm, press and pin in place. Stitch the hem close to the inner fold.

## Career Connections
The sewing and garment construction skills you're developing can lead to many exciting career opportunities in Ghana's fashion and textiles industries. Some possible career paths include:

- Tailor or Seamstress - sew custom-made clothing for individual clients
- Fashion Designer - design original clothing and accessories, create patterns, select fabrics, and oversee production
- Alterations Specialist - modify and repair garments for the perfect fit
- Textiles Engineer - develop new fabrics and manufacturing processes for garments and other textile products
- Production Manager - oversee clothing manufacturing in a factory setting
- Entrepreneur - start your own business as a fashion designer, clothing producer, or sewing teacher

## Key Vocabulary
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Seam allowance** | The distance between the stitching line and the raw fabric edge, usually 1-1.5cm |
| **Grain** | The direction of threads in a woven fabric - lengthwise (warp) and crosswise (weft) |
| **Pattern** | A template used for cutting fabric pieces to the correct size and shape |
| **Backstitch** | Sewing backward 3-4 stitches at the beginning and end of a seam to secure threads | 
| **Basting** | Long temporary stitches to hold fabric in place before permanent stitching |
| **Bias** | The diagonal direction across fabric, at a 45° angle to the grain |
| **Dart** | A wedge-shaped stitch used to shape fabric to fit body curves |
| **Facing** | A piece of fabric used to finish raw edges, like a neckline or armhole |
| **Interfacing** | A material fused to fabric to add stiffness, support and shape |
| **Appliqué** | Decorative technique of stitching small pieces of fabric onto a larger piece |
