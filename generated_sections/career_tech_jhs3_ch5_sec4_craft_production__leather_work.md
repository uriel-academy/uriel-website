## Learning Objectives
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
1. Identify different types of leather and their characteristics
2. Use appropriate tools and techniques to cut and shape leather
3. Apply leather finishing techniques like burnishing, painting, and waxing
4. Create a simple leather product like a wallet, pouch, or belt
5. Follow safety precautions when working with leather tools and materials

## Introduction
Leather crafting is an important traditional skill in Ghana with a long history. Many Ghanaians use leather goods in their daily lives, from shoes and belts to bags and furniture. Skilled leatherworkers can be found in workshops and markets across the country, creating beautiful handmade products. Learning leatherworking can open up career opportunities as an artisan, entrepreneur, or employee in the fashion and textile industries. With practice and creativity, you can make unique leather accessories to sell or use yourself.

Leatherwork also connects to Ghana's cultural heritage. For example, chiefs and important leaders often wear decorated leather sandals as a sign of their status. Leather is even used to make musical instruments like drums for cultural celebrations. By mastering this practical skill, you can express your creativity, start a small business, and connect to Ghana's rich artistic traditions.

## Main Content

### Types of Leather 
There are several types of leather used in crafting, each with different properties:

| Leather Type | Characteristics | Common Uses |
|--------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Full-grain   | Strong, durable, shows natural texture | High-quality shoes, belts, furniture |
| Top-grain    | Sanded and finished, smooth surface | Wallets, bags, jackets |  
| Split        | Suede-like texture, less durable | Gloves, cheaper shoes and bags |

In Ghana, goat, sheep, and cow leather are the most common. You can purchase leather from local tanners or leathercraft suppliers.

### Cutting and Shaping Leather
1. Create a paper pattern for your leather product. Measure carefully and add extra for seam allowance.
2. Place the pattern on the leather and trace around it with a pencil or scratch awl.
3. Use a sharp leather knife or rotary cutter to carefully cut out the leather pieces. Cut on a cutting mat and keep your fingers away from the blade.
4. If needed, use an edge beveler tool to skive (thin) the edges of the leather for a neater appearance.
5. Punch holes for stitching or add hardware like buckles.

[IMAGE: Demonstrating cutting leather following pattern lines] 

### Finishing Techniques
- **Burnishing**: Use an edge slicker tool and water to seal and polish the cut edges of the leather for a smooth finish.
- **Dyeing**: Apply leather dye with a sponge or cloth to change the color. Test on a scrap first and allow to dry fully.
- **Painting**: Use acrylic leather paints to add designs and patterns. Seal with a clear acrylic finisher.  
- **Waxing**: Rub beeswax into the leather as a final step to waterproof and add shine.

## Practical Activity
Make a simple coin pouch using scrap leather pieces.

Materials:
- Two 10x10cm pieces of leather 
- Waxed thread and stitching needle
- Snap closure or velcro

Instructions:
1. Cut the leather pieces to size.
2. Punch holes around three sides for hand-stitching.  
3. Stitch the pouch together, leaving the top edge open.
4. Attach the snap or velcro closure centered on the top.
5. Burnish the edges with an edge slicker and water.

## Key Vocabulary

| Term         | Definition                                                     |
|--------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------|
| Awl          | Pointed tool used to pierce holes in leather for stitching      |
| Burnishing   | Polishing and sealing the cut edges of leather                  |
| Grain        | The outer surface of leather with unique textures               |
| Hardware     | Metal accessories like buckles, snaps, and rivets               |
| Leather      | Durable material made from tanned animal hides or skins         |
| Skiving      | Using a knife to taper or thin leather edges                    |
| Stitching    | Sewing leather pieces together, usually with a sturdy waxed thread |
| Suede        | Leather with a napped, fuzzy surface made from the underside of hide |
| Tanning      | Treating animal skins with chemicals to preserve and soften them into leather |
| Waxing       | Applying beeswax or polish to waterproof and shine leather      |
