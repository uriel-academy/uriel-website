## Main Content
### Properties of Carbon
Carbon is a unique element with the following properties:
- It has an atomic number of 6 and is represented by the symbol C
- It can form four covalent bonds, allowing it to create a wide variety of compounds
- It exists in several forms, including diamond, graphite, and fullerenes
- It is the fourth most abundant element in the universe

### Formation of Carbon Compounds
Carbon atoms can bond with other elements to form compounds in two main ways:
1. Covalent bonding: Carbon shares electrons with other atoms to form stable compounds
2. Coordinate (dative) covalent bonding: Carbon accepts a lone pair of electrons from another atom

The versatility of carbon bonding allows for the formation of millions of different compounds, both organic and inorganic.

### Organic vs. Inorganic Compounds
Carbon compounds can be classified into two main categories:

| Organic Compounds | Inorganic Compounds |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| Contain carbon-hydrogen bonds | Do not contain carbon-hydrogen bonds |
| Found in living organisms | Found in non-living things |
| Examples: carbohydrates, proteins, fats | Examples: carbon dioxide, carbonate minerals |

### Examples of Organic Compounds
Organic compounds are essential for life and have numerous applications in our daily lives. Some examples include:

1. Carbohydrates (C<sub>x</sub>H<sub>y</sub>O<sub>z</sub>)
   - Sugars (e.g., glucose, C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub>)
   - Starch (found in cassava, yams, and plantains)
   - Cellulose (main component of plant cell walls)

2. Proteins (C, H, O, N, and sometimes S)
   - Enzymes (catalyze biochemical reactions)
   - Structural proteins (e.g., collagen)
   - Transport proteins (e.g., hemoglobin)

3. Fats and Oils (C, H, and O)
   - Energy storage
   - Insulation and protection
   - Examples: palm oil, shea butter

### Practical Example: Extracting Oils from Plants
You can extract oils from various plants using simple materials found at home:
1. Collect plant material (e.g., palm fruit, coconut, shea nuts)
2. Grind or crush the plant material
3. Boil the crushed material in water for 30 minutes
4. Allow the mixture to cool and skim off the oil that rises to the surface
5. Filter the oil through a clean cloth to remove any impurities

## Summary

## Key Vocabulary
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Carbon | A chemical element with the symbol C and atomic number 6 |
| Covalent bond | A chemical bond formed by the sharing of electrons between atoms |
| Organic compound | A chemical compound that contains carbon-hydrogen bonds |
| Inorganic compound | A chemical compound that does not contain carbon-hydrogen bonds |
| Carbohydrate | An organic compound composed of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen, often ending in "ose" |
| Protein | A complex organic compound composed of amino acids, containing carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, and sometimes sulfur |
| Fat | An organic compound composed of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen, characterized by long hydrocarbon chains |
| Hydrocarbon | A compound consisting of hydrogen and carbon atoms |
| Enzyme | A protein that catalyzes biochemical reactions |
| Fullerene | A form of carbon consisting of hollow spherical, elliptical, or cylindrical molecules
