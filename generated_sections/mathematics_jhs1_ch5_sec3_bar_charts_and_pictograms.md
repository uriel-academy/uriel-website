## Main Content

### What are Bar Charts and Pictograms?
**Bar charts** and **pictograms** are graphical representations of data that use bars or pictures to show the frequencies or values of different categories. They are used to compare and display data in a clear and visually appealing way.

In a bar chart, each category is represented by a rectangular bar, and the height or length of the bar is proportional to the value or frequency of that category. The bars can be vertical or horizontal, and they should be of equal width with equal spacing between them.

[IMAGE: A vertical bar chart showing the number of different fruits sold at a market stall over a week. The categories (fruits) are on the x-axis, and the frequencies (number sold) are on the y-axis.]

A pictogram, also known as a pictograph, uses pictures or symbols to represent data. Each picture or symbol corresponds to a specific value, and the number of pictures or symbols used for each category represents the frequency or value of that category.

[IMAGE: A pictogram showing the number of students who prefer different subjects. Each student icon represents 10 students, and the subjects are listed on the left side of the pictogram.]

### Interpreting Bar Charts and Pictograms
To interpret a bar chart or pictogram, follow these steps:
1. Read the title and labels to understand what data is being represented.
2. Identify the categories on the x-axis (horizontal axis) and the frequencies or values on the y-axis (vertical axis).
3. In a pictogram, determine the value represented by each picture or symbol.
4. Compare the heights of the bars or the number of pictures/symbols to determine which categories have the highest or lowest values.
5. Look for any patterns, trends, or differences between the categories.

### Constructing Bar Charts and Pictograms
To create a bar chart or pictogram, follow these steps:
1. Gather the data you want to represent and organize it into categories and frequencies or values.
2. Choose an appropriate scale for the y-axis (bar chart) or the value represented by each picture/symbol (pictogram). The scale should be consistent and easy to read.
3. Label the x-axis with the categories and the y-axis with the frequencies or values.
4. Draw the bars or add the pictures/symbols for each category according to the scale.
5. Add a title and any necessary labels or legends to make the chart easy to understand.

### Choosing Appropriate Scales
When creating a bar chart or pictogram, it is important to choose an appropriate scale. The scale should:
- Be consistent throughout the chart
- Start at zero (for bar charts)
- Have evenly spaced intervals
- Be easy to read and interpret
- Fit the data well without too much empty space or overcrowding

For example, if you are creating a bar chart showing the weights of different fruits in kilograms, you might use a scale that goes up by 0.5 kg intervals (0, 0.5, 1, 1.5, etc.).

| Fruit   | Weight (kg) |
|---------|-------------|
| Apple   | 0.2         |
| Banana  | 0.1         |
| Orange  | 0.3         |
| Pineapple | 1.5       |

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Interpreting a Bar Chart
The bar chart below shows the number of textbooks sold at a bookshop over five days.

[IMAGE: A vertical bar chart with the following data:
- Monday: 25 textbooks
- Tuesday: 40 textbooks 
- Wednesday: 35 textbooks
- Thursday: 20 textbooks
- Friday: 30 textbooks]

a) How many textbooks were sold on Wednesday?
   * From the bar chart, we can see that on Wednesday, 35 textbooks were sold.

b) On which day were the most textbooks sold?
   * By comparing the heights of the bars, we can see that the tallest bar corresponds to Tuesday, which means the most textbooks (40) were sold on Tuesday.

c) How many more textbooks were sold on Friday than on Thursday?
   * Thursday: 20 textbooks sold
   * Friday: 30 textbooks sold
   * Difference = 30 - 20 = 10 textbooks
   * Therefore, 10 more textbooks were sold on Friday than on Thursday.

### Example 2: Constructing a Pictogram
Create a pictogram to represent the following data about the number of students who prefer different subjects:
- Mathematics: 40 students
- Science: 30 students 
- English: 20 students
- Social Studies: 50 students

Let each student icon represent 10 students.

Step 1: Determine the number of icons needed for each subject.
- Mathematics: 40 ÷ 10 = 4 icons
- Science: 30 ÷ 10 = 3 icons
- English: 20 ÷ 10 = 2 icons  
- Social Studies: 50 ÷ 10 = 5 icons

Step 2: Draw the pictogram with the appropriate number of icons for each subject.
