## Introduction
Matter is all around us, from the air we breathe to the water we drink and the food we eat. In Ghana, we encounter various states of matter in our daily lives. For example, the cocoa beans harvested from farms are solid, while the Volta River that powers the Akosombo Dam is liquid. Even the steam rising from a hot plate of fufu is a form of matter. Understanding the different states of matter and how they change is essential for making sense of the world around us and applying this knowledge to various aspects of life, such as agriculture, energy production, and health.

## Main Content
### The Three States of Matter
Matter primarily exists in three states: solid, liquid, and gas. Each state has distinct characteristics that set it apart from the others.

**Solids** have a fixed shape and volume. The particles in a solid are closely packed and vibrate in fixed positions. Examples of solids in Ghana include rocks, wood, and cocoa beans.

**Liquids** have a fixed volume but take the shape of the container they are in. The particles in a liquid are close together but can move around each other. Examples of liquids in Ghana include water, palm oil, and honey.

**Gases** have no fixed shape or volume and will expand to fill the container they are in. The particles in a gas are far apart and move randomly. Examples of gases in Ghana include air, smoke from burning wood, and the natural gas used for cooking.

### Changing States of Matter
Matter can change from one state to another when energy is added or removed. These changes are called **phase changes**.

- **Melting**: When a solid is heated, it changes into a liquid. The particles gain enough energy to overcome the forces holding them in a fixed position and start moving more freely. An example is when cocoa butter melts during chocolate production.

- **Freezing**: When a liquid is cooled, it changes into a solid. The particles lose energy and start to vibrate in fixed positions. An example is when water freezes into ice in a refrigerator.

- **Evaporation**: When a liquid is heated, it changes into a gas. The particles gain enough energy to completely overcome the forces holding them together and move far apart. An example is when water evaporates from the surface of the Volta River.

- **Condensation**: When a gas is cooled, it changes into a liquid. The particles lose energy and start to move closer together. An example is when water vapor in the air condenses on a cold glass of sobolo (hibiscus tea).

### The Role of Energy in Changing States of Matter
Energy plays a crucial role in changing states of matter. When energy is added to a substance, the particles gain kinetic energy and move faster. This can cause the substance to change from a solid to a liquid or from a liquid to a gas. Conversely, when energy is removed from a substance, the particles lose kinetic energy and move slower. This can cause the substance to change from a gas to a liquid or from a liquid to a solid.

### Practical Example: Evaporation and Condensation in the Water Cycle
The water cycle is a perfect example of how states of matter change in nature. In Ghana, the water cycle plays a vital role in maintaining the balance of ecosystems and providing water for various human activities.

Experiment: You can observe evaporation and condensation using a simple setup with local materials.
Materials:
- A small pot or bowl
- Water
- A plate or lid that can cover the pot or bowl
- A heat source (e.g., stove, fire)

Procedure:
1. Fill the pot or bowl with water and place it on the heat source.
2. Heat the water until it starts to boil and produce steam.
3. Carefully place the plate or lid on top of the pot or bowl, allowing some space for the steam to escape.
4. Observe the steam rising from the boiling water (evaporation) and the droplets forming on the underside of the plate or lid (condensation).
5. Allow the setup to cool and observe the condensed droplets falling back into the pot or bowl (precipitation).

## Worked Examples
1. Converting units of temperature:
   - Problem: Convert 35°C to Kelvin.
   - Solution:
     * The formula to convert Celsius to Kelvin is: K = °C + 273.15
     * K = 35 + 273.15
     * K = 308.15
   - Therefore, 35°C is equal to 308.15 K.

2. Calculating the energy required to melt a substance:
   - Problem: Calculate the energy required to melt 100 g of ice at 0°C. The specific latent heat of fusion for water is 334 J/g.
   - Solution:
     * The formula to calculate the energy required is: Q = m × L
     * Q = energy required (J)
     * m = mass of the substance (g)
     * L = specific latent heat of fusion (J/g)
     * Q = 100 g × 334 J/g
     * Q = 33,400 J
   - Therefore, 33,400 J of energy is required to melt 100 g of ice at 0°C.

## Summary
- Matter exists in three main states: solid, liquid, and gas.
- Solids have a fixed shape and volume, liquids have a fixed volume but take the shape of their container, and gases have no fixed shape or volume.
- Matter can change states through processes such as melting, freezing, evaporation, and condensation.
- Energy plays a crucial role in changing states of matter. Adding energy can cause a substance to change from a solid to a liquid or from a liquid to a gas, while removing energy can cause the reverse changes.
- The water cycle is a practical example of how states of matter change in nature, involving evaporation, condensation, and precipitation.
- Understanding states of matter and their changes is essential for various aspects of life in Ghana, including agriculture, energy production, and health.

## Key Vocabulary
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Matter | Anything that has mass and takes up space |
| Solid | A state of matter with a fixed shape and volume |
| Liquid | A state of matter with a fixed volume but no fixed shape |
| Gas | A state of matter with no fixed shape or volume |
| Phase change | The process of matter changing from one state to another |
| Melting | The change of state from a solid to a liquid |
| Freezing | The change of state from a liquid to a solid |
| Evaporation | The change of state from a liquid to a gas |
| Condensation | The change of state from a gas to a liquid |
| Kinetic energy | The energy of motion |
| Latent heat | The energy absorbed or released during a phase change |
| Specific latent heat of fusion | The energy required to change a substance from a solid to a liquid |
| Specific latent heat of vaporization | The energy required to change a substance from a liquid to a gas |
| Precipitation | The process of water vapor condensing and falling back to Earth as rain, snow, or hail |
