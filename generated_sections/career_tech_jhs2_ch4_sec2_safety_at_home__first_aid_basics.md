## Main Content
### Common Household Injuries
Some of the most frequent injuries that occur at home include:
- Cuts and scrapes from knives, tools, or falls
- Burns from hot liquids, stoves, or fires
- Sprains and fractures from falls or heavy lifting
- Insect bites and stings
- Choking on food or small objects

### Basic First Aid Procedures
When someone is injured, remember these basic steps:
1. Stay calm and assess the situation
2. Call for help if needed (ambulance, fire service, or police)
3. Ensure the scene is safe before approaching the injured person
4. Provide immediate care based on the type of injury

Here are some common first aid procedures:

**Cleaning and dressing wounds:**
1. Wash your hands thoroughly with soap and clean water
2. Clean the wound gently with clean water and mild soap, removing any dirt or debris
3. Apply pressure with a clean cloth to stop bleeding
4. Cover the wound with a sterile adhesive bandage or gauze pad
5. Change the dressing daily and watch for signs of infection

[IMAGE: Step-by-step demonstration of cleaning and dressing a minor cut]

**Treating burns:**
1. Remove the person from the source of the burn (heat, chemicals, or electricity)
2. Cool the burn under running water for 10-15 minutes
3. Cover the burn loosely with a clean, dry cloth or bandage
4. Do not apply ice, butter, or any creams to the burn
5. Seek medical help for severe burns or burns on the face, hands, or genitals

[IMAGE: Illustration of proper burn treatment steps]

**Immobilizing fractures:**
1. Keep the injured area still and supported
2. Apply a splint or sling using available materials (wooden boards, cloth, or cardboard)
3. Secure the splint above and below the injured area
4. Check circulation and watch for signs of shock (pale, cold skin; rapid breathing)
5. Transport the person to a medical facility for proper treatment

| Injury | First Aid Materials |
|--------|------------|
| Cuts and scrapes | Clean water, soap, clean cloth, adhesive bandages or gauze |  
| Burns | Clean water, clean dry cloth or bandage |
| Fractures | Splint materials (wooden boards, cloth, cardboard), triangular bandage for sling |

### Assembling a First Aid Kit
Every home, school, and workplace should have a well-stocked first aid kit. Here are some essential items to include:

- Adhesive bandages (plasters) in various sizes
- Sterile gauze pads and rolls
- Triangular bandage for slings or splints
- Scissors and tweezers
- Safety pins
- Disposable gloves
- Mild soap or antiseptic solution
- Oral rehydration salts (ORS) for treating diarrhea
- Pain relief medication (paracetamol or ibuprofen)
- Antihistamine for allergic reactions
- Insect bite cream
- First aid manual or quick reference guide

## Practical Activity
1. **Make Your Own First Aid Kit**
   - Collect the necessary items from local pharmacies, supermarkets, or online retailers
   - Organize the items in a clear plastic container or sturdy bag
   - Label the container clearly as a first aid kit
   - Store the kit in an easily accessible location at home
   - Check and restock the kit regularly, replacing expired items

2. **First Aid Role-Play Scenarios**
   - Divide the class into small groups
   - Assign each group a different first aid scenario (cut, burn, fracture, choking)
   - Have the groups practice the appropriate first aid techniques using their homemade first aid kits
   - Discuss the steps taken and provide feedback on proper procedures
   - Emphasize the importance of staying calm, assessing the situation, and seeking help when needed

## Key Vocabulary
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| First aid | Immediate care given to an injured or sick person before professional medical treatment is available |
| Wound | An injury that involves a break in the skin or other body tissues |
| Sterile | Free from bacteria, viruses, and other microorganisms that can cause infection |
| Adhesive bandage | A small, sterile dressing used to cover and protect minor wounds (also called a plaster) |
| Gauze | A thin, translucent fabric used for dressings and bandages |
| Splint | A rigid device used to immobilize and support a fractured or dislocated bone |
| Sling | A supporting bandage used to hold an injured arm or hand against the chest |
| Antiseptic | A substance that prevents the growth of bacteria and other microorganisms on skin or wounds |
| Oral rehydration salts (ORS) | A mixture of sugar, salt, and water used to treat dehydration caused by diarrhea |
| Antihistamine | A medication used to treat allergic reactions by blocking the effects of histamine |
| Tourniquet | A tight band used to stop severe bleeding from a limb (should only be used as a last resort) |
