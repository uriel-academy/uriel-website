## Main Content
### Mendel's Laws of Inheritance
Gregor Mendel, known as the "father of modern genetics," discovered the fundamental laws of inheritance through his experiments with pea plants. He found that traits are controlled by **genes**, which are passed from parents to offspring. Mendel's two main laws are:

1. Law of Segregation: During the formation of **gametes** (eggs and sperm), the two copies of each gene separate, so each gamete carries only one copy of each gene.

2. Law of Independent Assortment: Different genes for different traits are inherited independently of each other.

### Dominant and Recessive Traits
Genes can have different versions, called **alleles**. Alleles can be either **dominant** or **recessive**. Dominant alleles are expressed when present in either one or two copies, while recessive alleles are only expressed when present in two copies.

A good example of this in Ghana is sickle cell anemia and malaria resistance:
| Genotype | Phenotype |
|----------|-----------|
| HbA HbA (normal hemoglobin) | No sickle cell trait, no malaria resistance |
| HbA HbS (sickle cell trait) | Sickle cell trait, malaria resistance |
| HbS HbS (sickle cell anemia) | Sickle cell anemia, malaria resistance |

In this case, the HbA allele is dominant, and the HbS allele is recessive.

### Practical Example: Blood Type Inheritance
In humans, blood type is determined by the ABO gene system. The alleles for this gene are IA, IB, and i. IA and IB are codominant, while i is recessive.

| Genotype | Blood Type |
|----------|------------|
| IA IA or IA i | A |
| IB IB or IB i | B |
| IA IB | AB |
| ii | O |

## Worked Examples
1. In cocoa plants, purple pods (P) are dominant to green pods (p). If a heterozygous purple-podded plant (Pp) is crossed with a green-podded plant (pp), what proportion of the offspring will have purple pods?

Step 1: Determine the parent genotypes
- Purple-podded plant: Pp
- Green-podded plant: pp

Step 2: Create a Punnett square
```
    P p
  +----
p | pP pp
p | pP pp
```

Step 3: Count the number of offspring with each genotype
- PP: 0
- Pp: 2
- pp: 2

Step 4: Calculate the proportion of offspring with purple pods (PP or Pp)
- Purple-podded offspring: 2 out of 4, or 1/2 (50%)

Therefore, 50% of the offspring will have purple pods.

2. In humans, free earlobes (F) are dominant to attached earlobes (f). If two heterozygous individuals (Ff) have children, what is the probability that their first child will have attached earlobes?

Step 1: Determine the parent genotypes
- Both parents: Ff

Step 2: Create a Punnett square
```
   F f
  +----
F | FF Ff
f | Ff ff
```

Step 3: Count the number of offspring with each genotype
- FF: 1
- Ff: 2
- ff: 1

Step 4: Calculate the probability of a child having attached earlobes (ff)
- Probability of ff: 1 out of 4, or 1/4 (25%)

Therefore, there is a 25% chance that their first child will have attached earlobes.

## Summary
- Inheritance is the process by which traits are passed from parents to offspring through genes.
- Mendel's laws of inheritance include the law of segregation and the law of independent assortment.
- Traits can be dominant or recessive, with dominant traits being expressed when present in one or two copies and recessive traits being expressed only when present in two copies.
- Monohybrid crosses involve the inheritance of a single trait, while dihybrid crosses involve the inheritance of two traits.
- Punnett squares are used to predict the probability of offspring having certain traits based on the genotypes of the parents.
- Examples of inherited traits in humans include sickle cell anemia, which provides resistance to malaria, and blood type, which is determined by the ABO gene system.

## Key Vocabulary
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Gene | A unit of heredity that is transferred from parent to offspring and determines some characteristic of the offspring |
| Allele | One of the alternative forms of a gene that can occur at a given locus |
| Genotype | The genetic makeup of an organism, typically with reference to a specific character under consideration |
| Phenotype | The observable characteristics of an individual resulting from the interaction of its genotype with the environment |
| Dominant | An allele that is expressed in the phenotype when present in either one or two copies |
| Recessive | An allele that is only expressed in the phenotype when present in two copies |
| Homozygous | Having two identical alleles for a particular gene |
| Heterozygous | Having two different alleles for a particular gene |
| Monohybrid cross | A genetic cross involving a single trait controlled by one gene |
| Dihybrid cross | A genetic cross involving two traits controlled by two genes |
| Punnett square | A diagram used to predict the genotypes and phenotypes of offspring in a genetic cross |
| Codominance | A relationship between two alleles of a gene in which both alleles are expressed in the phenotype |
| Incomplete dominance | A relationship between two alleles of a gene in which the phenotype of the heterozygote is intermediate between the homozygous dominant and homozygous recessive phenotypes |
| Segregation | The separation of alleles during the formation of gametes |
| Independent assortment | The random distribution of the alleles of two or more genes into gametes, such that the alleles of one gene segregate independently of the alleles of other genes |
