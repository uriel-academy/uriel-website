## Main Content

### What is Sound?
Sound is a form of energy produced by vibrations. When an object vibrates, it causes the surrounding medium (such as air, water, or solid materials) to compress and expand, creating sound waves. These waves travel through the medium, carrying energy from the source of the vibration to our ears or other receivers.

### How Sound Travels
Sound waves require a medium to travel through. In Ghana, we most commonly experience sound traveling through air, but it can also travel through liquids and solids. The speed of sound varies depending on the medium:

| Medium      | Speed of Sound (m/s) |
|-------------|----------------------|
| Air         | 343                  |
| Water       | 1,481                |
| Steel       | 5,960                |

Sound waves travel fastest through solids because the molecules in solids are closely packed together, allowing vibrations to be transmitted more efficiently.

### Properties of Sound Waves
Sound waves have three main properties: frequency, amplitude, and wavelength.

1. **Frequency** is the number of vibrations per second, measured in hertz (Hz). Higher frequencies produce higher-pitched sounds, while lower frequencies produce lower-pitched sounds.

2. **Amplitude** is the maximum displacement of the particles in the medium from their resting position. It determines the loudness of the sound. Larger amplitudes result in louder sounds, while smaller amplitudes result in softer sounds.

3. **Wavelength** is the distance between two consecutive compressions or rarefactions in a sound wave. It is related to frequency by the equation:

   $v = f \lambda$

   where $v$ is the speed of sound in the medium, $f$ is the frequency, and $\lambda$ is the wavelength.

### Sound and the Human Ear
The human ear is an amazing organ that allows us to perceive sound. When sound waves enter the ear canal, they cause the eardrum to vibrate. These vibrations are then transmitted through the middle ear bones (the malleus, incus, and stapes) to the inner ear, where they are converted into electrical signals by the cochlea. The brain then interprets these signals as sound.

[IMAGE: Diagram of the human ear with labeled parts]

The human ear can typically hear sounds with frequencies between 20 Hz and 20,000 Hz. However, exposure to loud sounds (above 85 decibels) for extended periods can cause hearing damage. It is important to protect your ears by wearing earplugs or noise-canceling headphones in noisy environments, such as near heavy machinery or during loud music performances.

### Practical Example: Measuring the Speed of Sound
You can measure the speed of sound using a simple experiment with local materials:

1. Find a large open space, such as a football field or a park.
2. Have a friend stand at a measured distance (e.g., 100 meters) from you.
3. Clap your hands loudly, and have your friend start a stopwatch when they see you clap.
4. When your friend hears the sound of the clap, they should stop the stopwatch.
5. Record the time it took for the sound to reach your friend.
6. Calculate the speed of sound using the equation: $v = d / t$, where $v$ is the speed of sound, $d$ is the distance, and $t$ is the time.

Repeat the experiment several times and take the average of your results for a more accurate measurement.

## Worked Examples

1. Calculate the wavelength of a sound wave with a frequency of 440 Hz traveling through air (speed of sound in air = 343 m/s).

   $v = f \lambda$
   $343 = 440 \lambda$
   $\lambda = 343 / 440 = 0.78 \text{ m}$

   The wavelength of the sound wave is 0.78 meters.

2. A sound wave traveling through water has a wavelength of 1.5 meters and a frequency of 1000 Hz. Calculate the speed of sound in water.

   $v = f \lambda$
   $v = 1000 \times 1.5 = 1500 \text{ m/s}$

   The speed of sound in water is 1500 meters per second.

## Summary
- Sound is a form of energy produced by vibrations and travels through mediums as waves
- Sound waves require a medium (air, water, or solid materials) to travel through
- The speed of sound varies depending on the medium, with sound traveling fastest through solids
- Sound waves have three main properties: frequency (pitch), amplitude (loudness), and wavelength
- The human ear perceives sound when vibrations are transmitted through the ear canal, middle ear, and inner ear, and then interpreted by the brain
- Exposure to loud sounds can cause hearing damage, so it is important to protect your ears in noisy environments
- The speed of sound can be measured using a simple experiment with local materials

## Key Vocabulary

| Term        | Definition                                                                                 |
|-------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Sound       | A form of energy produced by vibrations                                                    |
| Vibration   | The rapid back-and-forth motion of an object                                               |
| Medium      | A substance (such as air, water, or solid materials) through which sound waves can travel  |
| Sound wave  | A pressure wave that carries sound energy through a medium                                 |
| Frequency   | The number of vibrations per second, measured in hertz (Hz)                                |
| Amplitude   | The maximum displacement of particles in a medium from their resting position              |
| Wavelength  | The distance between two consecutive compressions or rarefactions in a sound wave          |
| Pitch       | The perception of a sound's frequency, with higher frequencies sounding higher in pitch     |
| Loudness    | The perception of a sound's amplitude, with larger amplitudes sounding louder              |
| Cochlea     | The part of the inner ear that converts vibrations into electrical signals                 |
| Decibel     | A unit used to measure the intensity of sound, with higher decibels indicating louder sounds|
| Compression | A region in a sound wave where particles are closer together than normal                   |
| Rarefaction | A region in a sound wave where particles are farther apart than normal                     |
