## Main Content

### Structure and Function of DNA
**DNA**, or deoxyribonucleic acid, is the hereditary material found in all living organisms. It is a long, double-stranded molecule that resembles a twisted ladder, known as a **double helix**. The "rungs" of this ladder are made up of four chemical bases: adenine (A), thymine (T), guanine (G), and cytosine (C). These bases pair up in a specific way: A always pairs with T, and G always pairs with C.

[IMAGE: Diagram of DNA double helix structure, showing the sugar-phosphate backbone and the complementary base pairs (A-T and G-C)]

The sequence of these bases along the DNA molecule determines the genetic information for an organism. A specific segment of DNA that contains the instructions for a particular trait or characteristic is called a **gene**.

### DNA Replication and Cell Division
Before a cell divides, it must make an exact copy of its DNA in a process called **DNA replication**. This process ensures that each new cell has a complete set of genetic instructions. The steps of DNA replication are as follows:

1. The double helix unwinds and separates into two single strands.
2. Each single strand serves as a template for building a new complementary strand.
3. Free nucleotides (A, T, C, and G) pair up with their complementary bases on the template strands.
4. DNA polymerase enzymes join the nucleotides together, forming new strands.
5. The result is two identical double helix DNA molecules.

### Inheritance of Genetic Information
Genetic information is passed from parents to offspring through **inheritance**. Each parent contributes one set of genes to their offspring, resulting in the offspring having two copies of each gene, one from the mother and one from the father. The different versions of a gene are called **alleles**.

| Inheritance Pattern | Description | Example |
|------------|-------------|---------|
| Dominant   | One allele masks the expression of the other | Tall plant height (T) is dominant over short plant height (t) |
| Recessive  | Both alleles must be present for the trait to be expressed | Sickle cell anemia requires two recessive alleles (ss) |
| Codominant | Both alleles are expressed equally | ABO blood types (A, B, AB, O) |

### Mutations and Genetic Variation
**Mutations** are changes in the DNA sequence of an organism. They can be caused by factors such as UV radiation, chemicals, or errors during DNA replication. Mutations can have various effects on an organism, ranging from no effect to minor changes in traits to major effects on health and survival.

Types of Mutations:
- **Point mutations**: Changes in a single base pair (e.g., sickle cell anemia)
- **Insertions**: Addition of one or more base pairs into the DNA sequence
- **Deletions**: Removal of one or more base pairs from the DNA sequence

Mutations are a source of **genetic variation**, which is essential for the process of **evolution**. Genetic variation allows organisms to adapt to changing environments and can lead to the development of new species over time.

### Genetic Diversity in Agriculture
Genetic diversity is crucial in agriculture, as it helps crops adapt to various environmental conditions and resist diseases. In Ghana, cocoa farming is a major agricultural industry, and maintaining genetic diversity in cocoa plants is essential for the industry's sustainability.

Strategies for maintaining genetic diversity in cocoa farming:
1. Planting a variety of cocoa genotypes
2. Preserving wild cocoa populations
3. Using traditional farming practices that promote diversity
4. Developing new cocoa varieties through selective breeding or genetic engineering

## Worked Examples

Example 1: Calculating the Probability of Offspring Inheriting a Trait
A man with type A blood (genotype AO) marries a woman with type B blood (genotype BO). Calculate the probability of their offspring having type AB blood.

Step 1: Set up the Punnett square.
```
    A O
  +----
B | AB BO
O | AO OO
```

Step 2: Count the number of offspring with type AB blood (1) and the total number of possible offspring (4).

Step 3: Calculate the probability.
Probability = Number of offspring with type AB blood รท Total number of possible offspring
Probability = 1 รท 4 = 0.25 or 25%

Therefore, there is a 25% chance that their offspring will have type AB blood.

Example 2: Determining the Genotype of an Offspring
A tall pea plant (genotype TT) is crossed with a short pea plant (genotype tt). All the resulting offspring are tall. What is the genotype of the offspring?

Step 1: Set up the Punnett square.
```
    T T
  +----
t | Tt Tt
t | Tt Tt
```

Step 2: Observe that all the offspring have the genotype Tt.

Therefore, the genotype of the offspring is Tt (heterozygous tall).

## Summary

- Genes are segments of DNA that code for specific traits or characteristics.
